2. Ask questions

Your questions should have two goals:

1. to get more and specific information

2. to uncover the other party's needs

Questions may be open-ended and expansive or closed-ended and restrictive.

  1. Closed-ended questions These questions can be answered either as yes or no. You use this method of questioning to get specific information that guides the discussion or conversation more to your favour.
  2. Open-ended questions They do not lead the other party in any specific direction. Generally speaking, they reveal much more about the counterpart's feelings and needs than restrictive questions do.

Explore five keys of proper questioning:

1. Have a plan

Ask yourself what type of information will help you make the right decision. Do you have to be more direct or indirect?

2. Know the other person

The more you know about your negotiation partner, the better you can frame your questions.

3. Broad to narrow

Begin your questioning with broad questions and gradually bring it down to more specific answers.

4. Sense of timing

Be aware of your counterpart's feelings. If he finds a question offensive, you won't get any answers. Asking someone how his or her diet is going while she is eating a piece of pie is a good example of bad timing.

5. Ask for permission

Ask permission before asking a question. You counterpart is more likely to give you a complete answer if you ask him politely.
